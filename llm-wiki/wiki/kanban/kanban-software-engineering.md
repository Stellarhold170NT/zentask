# Kanban in Software Engineering

Kanban is an evolutionary change method and a pull system rooted in lean manufacturing that has been increasingly adopted in software engineering to achieve continuous development and delivery of value. It is often distinguished between **Kanban** (the change method) and **kanban** (the pull system).

## Core Principles and Practices

The Kanban method, as formulated for software development (notably by David Anderson), typically involves five to six core practices:

1.  **Visualize the Workflow**: Using physical or virtual boards and cards to represent work items and their movement through different states (e.g., Planned, In Progress, Done).
2.  **Limit Work-In-Progress (WIP)**: Implementing explicit constraints on the number of items allowed in each stage of the workflow to prevent bottlenecks and multitasking.
3.  **Manage and Measure Flow**: Using metrics such as lead time, cycle time, throughput, and queue size to optimize the delivery process. Common tools include Cumulative Flow Diagrams (CFDs) and Value Stream Maps.
4.  **Make Process Policies Explicit**: Establishing clear 'entry' and 'exit' criteria for work items moving between states.
5.  **Implement Feedback Loops**: Regular reviews and coordination points to ensure continuous alignment and improvement.
6.  **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)**: Using theoretical models (e.g., Theory of Constraints, Systems Thinking) to identify and act on improvement opportunities.

## Lean Roots

Kanban is one of the primary tools used to implement **Lean Thinking** in software development, which is guided by five interlinked concepts:

-   **Value**: Defined by the end customer.
-   **Value Stream**: Mapping all steps in the process to identify value-adding actions.
-   **Flow**: Ensuring work moves through the system without interruption or delay.
-   **Pull**: Starting work only when there is demand (customer pull).
-   **Perfection**: Striving for continuous improvement.

## Benefits and Challenges

### Claimed Benefits
-   **Reduced Lead Time and Cycle Time**: By limiting WIP and optimizing flow, products reach customers faster.
-   **Improved Quality and Predictability**: Visualizing the process helps identify defects and bottlenecks early.
-   **Flexibility and Responsiveness**: Allows teams to respond more effectively to dynamic market changes compared to traditional or some sprint-based agile methods.
-   **Increased Transparency**: Stakeholders and team members have a clear view of the work status.

### Reported Challenges
-   **Lack of Clear Definition**: The variety of interpretations (agile vs. lean vs. hybrid) can create confusion during implementation.
-   **Organizational Culture**: Lean thinking often requires significant shifts in organization culture and mindset.
-   **Social and Contextual Complexity**: Unlike manufacturing, software engineering is highly socially embedded and complex, making "out-of-the-box" implementation difficult.

## State of Research

Research into Kanban in software engineering has grown significantly since 2010. While early literature was often anecdotal or consultancy-driven, more recent systematic reviews and mapping studies have begun to provide a more rigorous basis for its effectiveness. Adoption in the industry has shown a marked increase, with reports indicating a rise from 31% to 50% between 2015 and 2016.
